## The one-sentence answer

Give the result in one ordinary-language sentence, including uncertainty.

## First, the idea in everyday language

Assume the reader barely knows what a neural network or language model is.
Begin with a concrete analogy and explain the problem, proposed idea, and
meaning of success before introducing mathematical or project terminology.

## Why this question matters

Explain what becomes possible if the idea works and what decision this report
supports. Connect the tiny experiment to the long-term language goal without
claiming that the tiny experiment already solves it.

## What we tested

Describe the data as examples a person can imagine, the systems, what each one
can see, what changes between conditions, seeds, scale, and stopping rule.

## What a fair comparison means here

Explain matched information, compute, tuning, controls, exclusions, and how
leakage or an easier candidate set could create a false success.

## What happened

Present an intuitive table before detailed metrics. State sample counts and
uncertainty. Keep failed and invalid runs visible.

## The intuitive picture

Include at least one figure with an explanatory alt description and a caption
that tells the reader what to notice and why it matters.

## The technical details

Now define the architecture, targets, losses, hyperparameters, diagnostics,
statistical treatment, and exact evaluation protocol. Link manifests and raw
artifacts instead of omitting reproducibility details.

## What we can conclude

Separate direct observations from the inference supported by them.

## What we cannot conclude

List failed validity gates, alternative explanations, scale limitations, and
claims that remain unsupported.

## What happens next

Explain the decision, smallest next question, expected result patterns, and
scale gate.
